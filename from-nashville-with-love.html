From Nashville, With Love

A tornado decimated our town. Now, we’re fighting a pandemic.

whitney pastorek
Mar 17 · 6 min read

Imagine for a second that you wake up one morning and your neighborhood has been destroyed. Houses flattened, trees vanished. The bars are gone or at least shuttered, as are the restaurants; your chiropractor and your yoga studio and the fancy wine shop and the pizza place and the hardware store and the plant shop and the liquor store and the rock clubs and the pet food store and the art supply store and the tea house are, too.

You can’t get to the dog park because the roads are blocked with fallen limbs and telephone poles, and the streets between you and your friends’ houses— the ones still standing—are barricaded by police; schools and day cares are closed because they are no longer safe for kids; it would take you an hour and a half to get to work, if you felt like it was safe to try. Helicopters and sirens echo in the distance; the phone dings constantly with messages from people who live in other cities and countries checking in to see if you’re okay. You’re pretty sure you are, but you’re not sure if or when or how things will ever return to normal.

That’s been life in East Nashville since the tornado came through in the midnight hours of March 3. That’s been life in Germantown, North Nashville, Donelson, Hermitage, Mt. Juliet, and Cookeville. This wallop of a disaster came through and blew it all away—homes, churches, every business listed above and lots more than that. We’ve been wandering around in the rubble looking for some way to help ever since. The majority of people who came to help two weeks ago just showed up without being asked. By the time the official helpers came, it felt like there was almost nothing left for them to do.

Our city, in this moment when we needed only to come together, now has to stay apart.

And if we didn’t have a chainsaw or a backhoe or a big truck or a crew of roofers or any applicable physical skills at all, we didn’t wait for instructions — we wandered around outside the rubble trying to do everything we could. We donated our own money and collected money from friends and delivered supplies to relief centers and stood outside the doors of those relief centers logging donations and directing traffic and making burgers and handing out sandwiches and pallets of water and staring at our phones and reposting someone else’s update about ways to help. We hoped every little bit mattered — and every little bit kinda did.

We were out of toilet paper by March 5, not because of the coronavirus, but because we donated it all. We donated all the cleaning products, disinfectant wipes, and paper towels. When it came time to panic shop a week later, there already wasn’t a whole lot left.

Now the universe blows through with this pandemic, with this virus that, not unlike a midnight tornado, can’t be seen or felt until it’s on top of us. It tells us that after two weeks of wandering around the rubble trying to help, that the best thing we can do is sit at home. After two weeks of trying to find our loved ones, that we can’t hug them when we do. After two weeks of panic shopping for others, we need to do it for ourselves. Our bars and restaurants, at least the ones that still have roofs and walls, have to close again, almost literally to the day they were planning to reopen. Our musicians have to postpone their benefit concerts, tours, and festivals. Our city, in this moment when we needed only to come together, now has to stay apart.

We’re doing it, by the way. That viral video you saw of idiots packed together in a downtown Nashville bar a few nights ago? That wasn’t anyone who lives here. Those were the tourists, the people who’ve taken this town and turned it into an upside-down world of Airbnbs and “transportainment,” where we can’t fully fund affordable housing or public transportation (or public schools or infrastructure repairs) because we don’t have the money to do those things, no matter how many bachelorette parties we host, how many NFL drafts show up and ask everyone who lives here to participate in some sort of bizarre exercise where we become walk-around characters in the Disneyworld of our own lives for the benefit of drunk people from somewhere else. We’re doing it all, everything that is asked of us. And we’ll keep doing it, and thanking you for the opportunity to do it, because what else is there to do, really?

Every car has become a taxi. Every block has become a tourist attraction.

This is a letter from a town that was struggling before the tornado, that was struggling during and after the tornado, and now has to struggle to fight a pandemic in the face of drunk idiots who show up to party and then come back to our neighborhoods and spread their idiocy around because every house has become a hotel, every car has become a taxi. Every block has become a tourist attraction. We are all wearing stuffed heads.

Someone needs to find a moose costume and stand at the airport and tell folks “sorry, the park’s closed.”

In the meantime, if you’ll spare a thought for Nashville, I’ll tell you what we’ve learned in the days since the storm, because it might be of some help to you now, facing a global storm of your own.

We’ve learned that sometimes the people who are supposed to be in charge are just as shell-shocked as everyone else, and if you know what the right thing is to do, it’s easier to just do it yourself rather than wait to be told.

We’ve learned that hoarding toilet paper is only okay if you’re giving it away to others. Same with food and cleaning supplies, tarps, and blankets—nothing is doing you any good if you’re keeping it only for yourself.

We’ve learned that meals taken to go, or served on the sidewalk or out of the back of a truck or from a stranger’s barbecue grill in a parking lot outside a relief center can taste just as good as the ones eaten in a restaurant.

We’ve learned that supporting small business is more important than the ease of shopping online, and if you can’t shop in person — whether because of a virus or because the small business is literally a pile of rubble right now — buying a gift certificate is a good way to help.

We’ve learned that it’s easier to stay home if you keep in mind that other people don’t have a home in which to stay.

We’ve learned that checking on your friends, your loved ones, and your community is imperative, even if it’s only by text. Last Sunday, 600 people showed up at an event designed for 300, just to go out into north Nashville and knock on doors to check on folks whose homes were destroyed. Now, instead of going on foot, we’re using technology to reach out. We will find ways to keep reaching out, in every way we can.

We’ve learned that we can survive some inconvenience if it makes life easier for someone else.

We’ve learned that it’s easier to stay home if you keep in mind that other people don’t have a home in which to stay.

We’ve learned that we’re all in this together. That our neighborhood, even if it’s gone, is more than bars, restaurants, and yoga studios. It’s us. Our neighborhood is you and me.

And when it’s all said and done — when the winds have died down and the limbs have been cut from the streets and the power has been restored and the churches have been repaired and the music once again spills out of the rock clubs onto packed sidewalks of drunk tourists who don’t have a care in the world — it’s going to take all of us to build whatever’s coming next.

Until then, be safe out there, Nashville — and everywhere else. Wash your hands. Don’t touch your face. Stay the fuck home for a bit. Let the storm pass. Look out for each other. We’re all we’ve got.
